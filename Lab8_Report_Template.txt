TITLE: OFDM System Analysis with Cyclic Prefix: Impact on Multipath Channels

================================================================================
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

This lab investigates the critical role of the Cyclic Prefix (CP) in protecting
OFDM systems against multipath fading. We build a complete OFDM system from first
principles, then systematically study how the CP and equalization transform a
distorted multipath channel into manageable per-subcarrier scalar channels.

OVERVIEW:
In Phase 2, we simulate an ideal AWGN channel (h = 1) with CP enabled. 
In Phase 3, we disable the CP and simulate the same multipath channel 
(h = [1, 0, 0, 0.6]) to showcase ISI without protection.

2. Reception Constellation at 20 dB SNR (Phase 3):
   - Red scatter plot showing smeared/distorted QPSK constellation
   - ISI causes symbols to spread away from ideal QPSK lattice points

Question for Report:
"Why does the BER floor exist in Phase 3 even with perfect H[k] knowledge?"

ANSWER (Exactly 1 sentence):
The BER floor exists because inter-symbol interference (ISI) corrupts the 
data irrecoverably due to non-circular convolution, and the one-tap equalizer 
cannot separate ISI-distorted symbols even with perfect channel knowledge.

OBSERVATIONS:
- The dramatic rise in BER for Phase 3 at high SNR demonstrates catastrophic 
  failure without CP protection.
- The flat BER floor is characteristic of ISI-dominated systems where noise 
  becomes negligible but interference dominates.
- This motivates the need for CP in multipath environments.

TODO 3: CYCLIC PREFIX LENGTH SWEEP
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
We fix Eb/N0 = 10 dB and sweep the CP length through {0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 32}
to find the optimal CP length for a long channel (h_long with tau ≈ 20).

SIMULATION PARAMETERS:
- Channel: h_long = [1, 0.9, 0.7, 0.5, 0.3, 0.2, 0.1] (decaying multipath, tau ~ 20)
- Eb/N0: Fixed at 10 dB
- Bits per iteration: 10^5
- Ncp sweep: {0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 32}

EXPECTED RESULTS:
- BER decreases rapidly as Ncp increases from 0 to 16-20.
- BER reaches a "knee" (optimal plateau) around Ncp = 20 (matching tau).
- For Ncp > 20, BER remains flat (no further improvement, only power penalty).

PLOTS GENERATED:
1. BER vs CP Length (semilogy):
   - Orange line with circular markers
   - Vertical dashed black line at Ncp = 20 (the tau value)
   - Text annotation: "Optimal CP = 20"
   - The knee identifies the minimum CP required to cover the channel delay spread.

DESIGN RULE:
Ncp >= tau (delay spread) ensures no ISI. Setting Ncp = tau is efficient; 
higher values waste power with minimal BER gains.

================================================================================
WHAT-IF: REMOVE THE EQUALIZER
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
We disable the one-tap equalizer (i.e., remove the division by H[k]) and observe
how raw received symbols are distorted per-subcarrier even with a working CP.

SYSTEM PARAMETERS:
- Channel: h_multipath = [1, 0, 0, 0.6]
- CP Length: 16 samples
- Eb/N0: 15 dB
- Modulation: QPSK

EXPECTED RESULTS:
- Received symbols are NOT clean QPSK points.
- Each subcarrier exhibits a different rotation and scaling (complex gain).
- Symbol cloud for each subcarrier is distorted but separable.

PLOTS GENERATED:
1. Received Symbol Scatter (no equalizer):
   - 8 different subcarriers plotted in 8 distinct colors
   - Background faint unit circle for reference
   - Each color-coded cloud shows how that subcarrier's channel gain 
     rotated/scaled ALL received symbols in that subcarrier.

REPORT QUESTION:
"Why does the Cyclic Prefix alone NOT produce clean QPSK symbols?
What specific job does the equalizer perform that the CP does not?
(≤ 5 sentences)"

ANSWER:

The Cyclic Prefix alone does not produce clean QPSK because while it converts 
linear convolution to circular convolution, allowing per-subcarrier scalar 
channels, each subcarrier is still rotated and scaled by its own complex 
channel gain H[k]. Without the one-tap equalizer (dividing by H[k]), the 
received symbols remain distorted in both magnitude and phase. The equalizer 
is essential to normalize and recover the original symbol constellation by 
inverting the per-subcarrier channel response. Thus, the CP enables tractable 
equalization by eliminating ISI, but equalization is required to produce 
clean QPSK. In summary: CP handles ISI; equalizer handles per-subcarrier 
fading.

================================================================================
CONCEPTUAL CHECKPOINT
================================================================================

REPORT QUESTION:
"Explain in your own words why the Cyclic Prefix turns a linear multipath 
channel into independent per-subcarrier scalar channels.
(≤ 5 sentences)"

ANSWER:

The Cyclic Prefix repeats the last Ncp samples of each OFDM symbol at its 
beginning, turning the linear time-domain convolution with the multipath 
channel into circular convolution. In the frequency domain (via DFT), circular 
convolution becomes per-subcarrier pointwise multiplication: R[k] = H[k] * X[k] + noise.
Thus, each subcarrier experiences only a scalar multiplicative distortion H[k], 
independent of other subcarriers, eliminating inter-carrier interference and ISI.
This transforms a complex MIMO-like problem (where one symbol affects many due 
to ISI) into N independent single-tap scalar channels, allowing simple 
per-subcarrier equalization and making OFDM practical for wideband multipath 
channels.

================================================================================
FINAL THOUGHTS FOR DISCUSSION SECTION
================================================================================

1. POWER TAX OF CP:
   The CP is a "silent guardian" of OFDM; it adds redundancy (copies NFFT-NCP
   samples) and reduces the fraction of useful data per symbol. For Ncp = 16 
   and NFFT = 64, the efficiency drops to 64/80 = 80%. This ~0.97 dB power 
   penalty is why Phase 4 sits ~1 dB to the right of theory.

2. SCALABILITY TO LONGER CHANNELS:
   For very long delay spreads (τ >> NFFT), we would need Ncp ≈ τ, which may 
   exceed practical limits. Advanced techniques (zero-forcing equalization, 
   channel shortening prefilters, or windowing) address this.

3. BLIND CHANNEL ESTIMATION:
   This lab assumed perfect H[k] knowledge (from training pilots or preamble).
   Realistic systems must estimate H[k], introducing additional overhead and 
   imperfection. The clean constellations in TODO 2 plots are idealized.

4. EXTENSION TO MIMO-OFDM:
   The CP principle scales to MIMO-OFDM, where each subcarrier becomes a MIMO 
   channel (matrix) instead of scalar. Per-subcarrier linear precoding and 
   MMSE equalization replace simple one-tap scaling.
